Shortlist Analysis

Toyota Land CruiserBest total fit

Best match when the decision is weighted toward durability, long-term ownership confidence, road-trip capability, poor-weather competence, and a vehicle that still feels special after the novelty wears off.

Subaru OutbackStrong practical case

The rational runner-up. It wins on ease, lower acquisition cost, efficiency, and daily convenience. It is the better choice if the goal is to minimize footprint and avoid paying for capability that may only be used occasionally.

Toyota 4Runner / Lexus GXAdjacent alternatives

Worth considering if Jon wants the Toyota truck-based ownership profile but wants either a more familiar SUV shape or a more luxury-leaning interior package.

Methodology

Use-case weightingThe model prioritizes the vehicle that covers the greatest range of plausible real-world needs without requiring a second car or a major compromise.
Ownership-risk adjustmentHigher value is assigned to platforms with strong long-term reputation, parts/service ecosystem, and resale confidence.
Regret-risk adjustmentWhen two vehicles are both practical, the model favors the one less likely to feel like an undershoot three years from now.
Bottom line: the Outback is a very good answer to the practical version of the question. The Land Cruiser is the stronger answer to the full version of the question.

This tool is for directional fit assessment only. It does not replace test drives, insurance quotes, financing math, or the emotional clarity that comes from seeing a Land Cruiser in a parking lot.
